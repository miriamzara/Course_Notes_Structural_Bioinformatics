\paragraph{Why study protein dynamics? \done}
An average structure alone is not sufficient to understand protein behaviour. Flexibility is a crucial aspect of protein function. 
Proteins are consistantly in motion, and their conformational changes are often essential for their biological activity, such as ligand binding, allosteric regulation, and catalysis.
Additionally, we can also use molecular dynamics simulations to better understand structure itself. For example,   1) to predict protein structure (with an ab-initio simulation, or starting from a candidate and refining it) 2) incorporate solvent effects into experimentally determined structures (those obtained with X-ray crystallography or NMR) to make them more realistic.


\paragraph{What is a force field and which terms are included in a typical force field?}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Bonded interactions: bond stretching (harmonic - breaking not allowed), angle bending (harmonic), torsional rotation (periodic);
    \item Non-bonded interactions: van der Waals (Lennard-Jones potential), electrostatic interactions (Coulomb's law);
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{What is typically the rate-limiting step of force field evaluation?}

The rate-limiting step in force field evaluation is usually the calculation of non-bonded interactions (van der Waals and electrostatic) because they grow quadratically with the number of atoms in the system,
while the bonded interactions grow linearly. In particular, the electrostatic interactions are the most computationally expensive, because the potential decays slowly with distance (like 1/r, while lennard-jones decays like $1/r^6$).
