\subsection{Evolution}

\paragraph{Why is protein structure generally more conserved than sequence?}
Because the three-dimensional structure of a protein is what determines its function, there is strong evolutionary pressure to maintain the structure. The sequence can tolerate more mutations as long as the overall fold and function of the protein are preserved. 

\paragraph{What is the relation between sequence similarity and structural similarity in biological proteins?}
Protein structure is generally more conserved than sequence in evolution.
Proteins that share $>\sim30\%$ of sequence identity almost always have significantly similar
folds. Below this threshold ($20-30\%$ - the "twilight zone"), structural similarity can still exist despite low sequence similarity.
Summarising: similar sequence implies similar structure, but similar structure does not imply
similar sequence.

\paragraph{What are homologous proteins? Explain the difference between paralogues and orthologues}
Homologous proteins are proteins that are believed to derive from a common evolutionary ancestor \textit{gene}. 
Of course, we can never be $100\%$ certain about this claim. Homology is always an \textit{inference}, based on the comparison of both sequence and structure.
\\
Homologous proteins are the results of \textbf{divergent evolution}: they have diverged from a common ancestor. The mechanism of divergence can be either a speciation event (orthologues) or a gene duplication event (paralogues). More in detail:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Orthologues} are homologous proteins found in different species that originated by a \textbf{speciation event}. Since they usually retain the same biological role, orthologues often have similar functions. For example, the same enzyme in human and mouse is typically an orthologue.
\item \textbf{Paralogues} are homologous proteins that originated by a gene duplication event within a genome (within a single species). After duplication, the two copies start to accumulate mutations and diverge, and the two proteins may acquire different functions. Therefore, paralogues are evolutionarily related, but they are not necessarily functionally equivalent. An example is the genes that do olfactory recognition: they differentiated from a commn ancestor gene and then each specialized in recognition of different olfactory molecules.
\end{itemize}
The term "homologous" must not be confused with "analogous", which instead defines proteins that have similar structure (and, sometimes, also similar function) but do not derive from a common ancestor. Analogues are the result of \textbf{convergent evolution}, which is the independent evolution of similar features from different ancestors.

\paragraph{How does evolution constrain protein structure?}
Evolution constrains protein structure in several ways. 
\begin{itemize}
\item First of all, the fold must be \textit{stable}, i.e. it must have $\Delta G < 0$ in physiological conditions, as we saw.
\item Second, the fold must be \textit{functional}, i.e. it must be able to perform a specific biological function,
\item Third, the protein must be able to \textit{fold} into the correct structure in a reasonable time compatible with cellular functionality (ca from $1\mu s$ up to $1$ ms), otherwise the organism would not survive.
\end{itemize}


\paragraph{Why we observe only a limited number of folds? Explain briefly structure evolution model.}
We definitely see the same folds all over the tree of life, and they are relatively few. The reason is found in evolution, but we should distinguish two layers
\begin{itemize}
\item First, there are the constraints we just mentioned: the fold must be stable, functional, and foldable. These constraints certainly limit the number of folds. Also, there has been an evolutionary selection of folds that are better scaffolds for active sites (fold competition).
\item But there is a second layer, and it is related to the complete history of how proteins evolved from small peptides. At the start of life, it is believed that there was just RNA and proteins where just small cofactors that helped RNA to duplicate.
It is believed that proteins started as small peptides. The important point is that there exists only a limited number of super-secondary structures that are able to fold autonomously and are stable (e.g. $\alpha$-hairpins and $\beta$-hairpins). These were the fundamental building blocks of the first proteins. Then proteins differentiated
through a series of of rather complex mechanisms that acted \textit{on the genes}, which go beyond simple \textit{oligomerization} (i.e. repetition) and also include other mechanisms called \textit{fusion}, \textit{circular permutation}, etc. So the fact that some folds appear common is because they come from the same, rather limited number of ancestral building blocks. 
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Structural classification systems}

\paragraph{What is a \textit{domain} and what is the \textit{basic complement of domains}?}
A domain is a subunit of a protein that can fold independently. It means that if we cut the protein at the boundary of the domain, the domain will still fold into a stable structure. A domain has its
own hydrophobic core and has relatively little interaction with the rest of the protein, making it structurally independent. 
With "basic complement of domains" we mean the set of all domains that are found in nature. It is believed that this set was already established at the time of the \textit{last universal common ancestor} (LUCA), and that all domains found in nature today are the same as those of the LUCA. 

\paragraph{What is domain-based structural classification?}
With domain-based classification we mean classification methods that are applied on \textit{structural domains}, rather than on whole protein chains. Examples
of such classification methods are SCOP and CATH. Classification is aimed at reconstructing the evolutionary history of proteins.
The reason for designing a classification method based on domains is that those are believed to be the fundamental blocks in evoution. Indeed, evolution is believed
to create new proteins by combining, duplicating, fusing, or rearranging domains. Therefore, it is often more meaningful to consider homology at the domain level rather than at the whole protein level. 
A famous quote by  Nobel laureate François Jacob is:
"Nature is a tinkener, not an inventor" in his famous essay "Evolution and Tinkering" (1977). This means that evolution does not create new proteins from scratch, but rather reuses existing domains and combines them in new ways.
With this, he means that Nature does a sort of "bricolage": it does not create out of nothing, but opportunistically rearranges and recombine existing elements. This is true from the molecular level (proteins) to the organismal level.
As another analogy, is like someone that builds a car by assembling together spare parts from different cars, or it is like a Lego game, where the same blocks can be arranged in ways that form very different final structures.

\paragraph{How can two domains with different evolutionary origins end up in a single protein?}

Two domains with different evolutionary origins can end up in the same protein because genes are not fixed, indivisible units. During evolution, pieces of DNA can be duplicated, fused, inserted, deleted, or recombined. Since many protein domains correspond to relatively compact and semi-independent structural units, these genetic rearrangements can produce proteins made of domains that originally came from different ancestral proteins.
The most important mechanism is \textbf{gene fusion}. Suppose that, originally, there are two separate genes:
\[
\text{Gene A} \rightarrow \text{Protein A with Domain A}
\]
\[
\text{Gene B} \rightarrow \text{Protein B with Domain B}
\]
A mutation or recombination event can place these two genes next to each other and remove the stop/start separation between them. The result is a new fused gene:
\[
\text{Gene A-B} \rightarrow \text{Protein with Domain A + Domain B}
\]
In this way, the new protein contains two domains that were originally present in two separate proteins.
This is possible because domains are often \textbf{modular}. A domain can fold into a stable structure on its own, or at least semi-independently. Therefore, when two domains are joined in the same polypeptide chain, they do not necessarily prevent each other from folding correctly. They can remain as two compact structural units connected by a flexible linker:
\[
\boxed{\text{Domain A}} - \text{linker} - \boxed{\text{Domain B}}
\]
The linker gives the two domains enough freedom to fold and move relative to each other. If this fusion creates a functional advantage, natural selection can preserve it.
For example, before fusion, two proteins may physically interact:
\[
\text{Protein A} + \text{Protein B} \rightarrow \text{complex}
\]
If they frequently need to work together, it can be advantageous to encode them in one single protein:
\[
\text{Protein A-B}
\]
This increases the local concentration of the two functional parts, makes their interaction more efficient, and can improve regulation because both domains are produced at the same time and in the same amount.
This explains why many multidomain proteins contain domains with different functions. One domain may bind DNA, another may bind ATP, another may bind a ligand, and another may interact with other proteins. These domains may have different evolutionary origins, but after fusion they become parts of the same molecular machine.
Several evolutionary mechanisms can generate this situation. \textbf{Gene fusion} occurs when two previously separate genes become a single gene, directly producing a protein containing domains from both ancestral proteins. \textbf{Domain insertion} occurs when the sequence encoding one domain is inserted into the gene encoding another protein. The inserted domain may appear inside a loop or between two structural regions of the original protein. \textbf{Exon shuffling}, especially in eukaryotes, can also create new domain combinations, because recombination can move exons around. If an exon encodes a domain or part of a domain, this can generate a protein with a new domain architecture. Finally, \textbf{gene duplication followed by divergence} can contribute: after duplication, one copy can accumulate mutations, specialize, or fuse with another gene.
The important conceptual point is that domains are \textbf{evolutionary modules}. Evolution does not always invent proteins from zero. More often, it reuses existing domains and recombines them in new ways. Therefore, two domains with different evolutionary origins can be found in one protein because evolution can physically combine the DNA segments that encode them. If the resulting multidomain protein folds correctly and provides a functional advantage, it can be maintained and optimized by natural selection.

\paragraph{Explain briefly how SCOP classifies protein structures.}
