\paragraph{What does the term 'ab initio prediction' mean?}
The term \emph{ab initio} (Latin for ``from the beginning'') refers to methods that predict the three-dimensional structure of a protein starting only from its amino acid sequence, without relying on experimentally determined homologous structures as templates,
and more in general without relying on the knowledge of other protein structures in general.

\paragraph{What is CASP and why is it important?}
CASP (Critical Assessment of Structure Prediction) is a community-wide experiment that evaluates the performance of protein structure prediction algorithms. 
The organizers distribute only the amino acid sequences of proteins whose experimental structures are not yet public. Participants submit predictions, and after a few months the results are discussed at a conference.
CASP has been held every two years since $1994$  and today comphrises several categories in addition to structure prediction, including disorder prediction and model quality assessment.
A particularly important subcategory of structure prediction is \textbf{free modelling or "ab initio" modelling}, where no reliable template (homologous structure) is available. 


\paragraph{How are predicted structures evaluated in CASP?}
The main metric used in CASP to evaluate the accuracy of predicted structures is the Global Distance Test Total Score (GDT-TS).
GDT-TS measures the percentage of residues in the predicted structure that are within a certain distance threshold from their corresponding residues in the experimentally determined structure. 
The score ranges from 0 to 100, with higher scores indicating better predictions.


\paragraph{What is Rosetta and how does it work?}
Rosetta is a template-free method that dominated the CASP competition for years, until it was surpassed by AlphaFold. 
It is based on the idea of \textbf{fragment assembly}. Works as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Split the sequence in segments of $9$ residues,
    \item Use MSA to find fragments of known structures that have similar sequence profiles,
    \item Assemble these fragments (coming from unrelated proteins) into a complete structure,
    \item Define a potential energy function -or \textit{scoring function}- that combines physical and statistical terms:
            $$
            P(structure| sequence) = P(structure) \times P(sequence | structure)
            $$
    \item Perform simulated annealing on this potential surface to find the global minima,
    \item Cluster together similar conformations of low energy,
    \item Select the broadest cluster as the native ensemble.
\end{enumerate}
Although Rosetta is often classified as an ab initio or de novo method, it is not a purely ab initio approach because it relies on fragment libraries and statistical information extracted from previously solved protein structures. Its main innovation is the combination of fragment assembly, Monte Carlo sampling, and knowledge-based scoring functions to efficiently explore conformational space.


\paragraph{How does Rosetta select the native conformation when it finds several structures with similar low energy?}
Rosetta typically generates thousands of candidate structures (called \emph{decoys}) during the fragment assembly and Monte Carlo search process. Often, many of these structures have very similar energies, making it difficult to identify the native conformation based solely on the energy score.
After generating the decoys, Rosetta groups structurally similar models into clusters and identifies the \textbf{most populated cluster}. The representative structure of the largest low-energy cluster is often selected as the final prediction. This strategy is based on the observation that random incorrect (although low in energy) conformations are usually scattered throughout conformational space, whereas native-like structures tend to accumulate in the same region.
In other words: \textbf{the native minimum is broader than any other minimum}
Thus, Rosetta does not simply choose the structure with the absolute lowest energy; instead, it combines \textbf{energy ranking} with \textbf{clustering analysis}, selecting structures that are both low in energy and frequently observed during the search. This improves robustness against inaccuracies in the \textit{scoring function} and increases the probability of identifying the native fold.



\paragraph{Why is the global minimum broader than any other minimum - both in the \textit{real} energy landscape and in the ROSETTA scoring function landscape?}
\smallskip\noindent
\begin{center}
    \includegraphics[width=0.3\textwidth]{figures/rosetta.png}
\end{center}
\textit{In the real energy landscape}, the native minimum is expected to be broader than other minima because the stability of the native state is dominated by long-range hydrophobic interactions. Hydrophobic residues tend to cluster in the protein core, and this
favorable packing can be maintained even when the structure undergoes small conformational changes. As a result, many conformations that are similar to the native structure have comparable energies, creating a wide basin in the energy landscape. The native conformation corresponds
to a sharp, deep drop in this sorrounding basin of low energy.
\newline\smallskip\noindent
\textit{Rosetta} uses an approximate scoring function based on statistical potentials and simplified physical models. This scoring function captures broad features such as hydrophobic burial quite well, but is less accurate in describing more specific interactions such as hydrogen bonds, electrostatic interactions, and van der Waals contacts. Consequently, the sharp energy drop associated with the exact native structure is partially obscured by noise.
However, it is still able to reconstruct the \emph{broad region} surrounding the native state, where many low-energy conformations accumulate. Therefore, native-like structures tend to form large clusters in the rosetta conformational space, whereas incorrect folds are usually represented by isolated minima with fewer nearby low-energy conformations.




\paragraph{How is ROSETTA scoring potential modelled? What differs between the "generation" and "evaluation" steps?}
Rosetta uses a scoring function based on Bayes' theorem to estimate the probability that a candidate structure is the correct fold for a given sequence:
$P(\text{structure}|\text{sequence}) \propto P(\text{structure})P(\text{sequence}|\text{structure})$.
It applies a two-step process: a \textbf{generation step} that produces candidate structures using a simplified scoring function, and an \textbf{evaluation step} that assesses these candidates with a more detailed scoring function.
During structure generation, Rosetta requires a scoring function that is extremely fast because millions of candidate conformations must be evaluated. Therefore it uses a simplified statistical potential:
\[
P(\text{structure}|\text{sequence})
\approx
e^{-R_g^2}
\prod_{i<j}
\frac{P(r_{ij}|aa_i,aa_j)}
     {P(r_{ij})}
\]
which contains two main terms:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Radius of gyration term $P(\text{structure}) \sim e^{-R_g^2}$: which favors compact conformations and discriminates folded proteins from random coils. This depends on structure only, i.e. it is a prior distribution.
    \item Pairwise distance potential: $\frac{P(r_{ij}|aa_i,aa_j)} {P(r_{ij})}$:  which measures how likely it is to observe two amino acids at distance \(r_{ij}\). This is a statistical term.
\end{enumerate}
\smallskip \noindent Importantly, the simplified model assumes independence between residue pairs and ignores environmental effects, i.e. whether the residue pair is solvent-exposed or buried.
Once a smaller set of candidate structures has been generated, Rosetta can afford a more accurate scoring function which accounts for the environment:
the potential has a different shape if the residue pair is buried in the protein core or exposed to solvent. 

\paragraph{What is the pipeline of AlphaFold 1, and how does it differ from AlphaFold 2?}
AlphaFold 1 (CASP13, 2018) and AlphaFold 2 (CASP14, 2020) both use deep learning, but their pipelines are fundamentally different.
\smallskip \noindent AlphaFold 1 first takes a Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA) as input and uses a deep neural network to predict geometric constraints between residues, mainly residue--residue distance distributions and backbone angles. These predictions are converted into a potential function, which is then optimized using a separate structure-generation procedure based on gradient descent and energy minimization. Thus, the network does not directly output the final 3D structure; instead, it predicts constraints that are subsequently used by an optimization algorithm to build the model.
\smallskip \noindent In contrast, AlphaFold 2 is an end-to-end architecture. Starting from the MSA and pairwise residue information, the Evoformer module iteratively refines internal representations using attention mechanisms. The Structure Module then directly generates 3D atomic coordinates through Invariant Point Attention (IPA). The predicted structure is fed back into the network through a recycling procedure, allowing multiple rounds of refinement. Therefore, AlphaFold 2 learns the entire mapping from sequence to structure within a single differentiable framework.

\paragraph{Why does AlphaFold predict inter-residue distance distributions?}
A residue contact map contains only binary information: two residues are either considered in contact or not, typically using
a distance threshold. While useful, this representation loses a large amount of geometric information because many different protein structures can satisfy the same set of contacts.
AlphaFold instead predicts a probability distribution over possible distances for each pair of residues. This provides much richer structural information. Distance distributions have several advantages:
\begin{itemize}
    \item They specify not only whether two residues interact, but also how far apart they are likely to be.
    \item They provide more constraints for reconstructing the 3D structure, reducing ambiguity.
    \item They express uncertainty naturally: the network can assign probabilities to multiple possible distances.
    \item They can be converted into smooth potentials that guide structure optimization more effectively than binary contacts.
\end{itemize}
These predictions are converted into a differentiable scoring function (or potential), and a separate optimization procedure is used to construct the final structure.
The optimization starts from an initial protein conformation and iteratively modifies the atomic coordinates using gradient descent. At each step, the gradient of the scoring function with respect to the coordinates is computed, and the coordinates are adjusted in the direction that decreases the score.
The minimization objective is not a physical energy in the traditional molecular mechanics sense. Rather, it is a learned potential derived from the network predictions. The objective function rewards structures whose geometry agrees with the predicted distance distributions and angle constraints while penalizing violations of protein stereochemistry.


\paragraph{How can MSA be used to predict contact maps?}
The key idea is that residues that interact in the three-dimensional structure tend to co-evolve. If a mutation occurs at one position, a compensatory mutation may occur at another position to preserve the favorable interaction. These correlated mutations can be detected by analyzing a Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA) of homologous proteins.
To exploit this information, one computes correlations between columns of the MSA. Pairs of positions showing strong co-variation are candidates for being in contact in the folded protein. 
This approach was a major breakthrough in protein structure prediction because it allows structural information to be extracted directly from sequence databases, even when no experimental structure or template is available.

AlphaFold did not invent the use of MSAs for contact prediction. The idea of extracting structural information from co-evolution in MSAs was already a major field of research for about a decade before AlphaFold.
What AlphaFold did was use deep learning to exploit that information much more effectively and ultimately predict full 3D structures rather than just contacts.


\paragraph{What are the main inputs used by AlphaFold2 and what differs from AlphaFold1?}
AlphaFold 2 still uses MSA as a primary input, but also incorporates additional information:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The pair representation: is an $N \times N$ matrix in which AlphaFold stores a \textit{learned} description of the relationship between every pair of residues. It can be viewed as a rich, differentiable generalization of a contact map. Every entry is a vector. Basically, it is an embedding, like done in 
    large language models. This matrix then goes through the transformer and is updated at every step (like the buffer in a LLM).
    \item  When available, structural templates are also used.
\end{itemize}


\paragraph{What is the pipeline of AlphaFold 2?}
The pipeline can be divided into four main stages:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Database search for input generation: MSA and templates.
    \item Construction of \textit{internal} representations: MSA representation and pair representation.
    \item Evoformer: a large transformer-based network that processes the internal representations.
    \item Structure Module: takes as input the internal representations and generates 3D coordinates using Invariant Point Attention (IPA).
    \item Refinement: molecular dynamics simulation to reduce steric clashes.
    \item Recycling: the output of the structure module is fed back into the Evoformer for multiple rounds of refinement.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{What are the main outputs produced by AlphaFold2 besides the predicted structure?} These are the PAE and the pLDDT.
The PAE is a matrix of size $N \times N$ in which the entry $(i,j)$ estimates the expected positional error of residue $i$ when the predicted structure is aligned on residue $j$. This is especially useful for multidomain proteins, where the relative orientation of domains may be uncertain even if each domain is predicted accurately.
\includegraphics[width=0.2\textwidth]{figures/AF2_PAE.png}
The pLDDT (predicted Local Distance Difference Test) is a per-residue confidence score that estimates the reliability of the predicted structure at each position. High pLDDT values indicate high confidence, while low values suggest uncertainty or potential disorder in that region of the protein.

\paragraph{What is the Evoformer, and what differs between axial and triangular attention?}

The Evoformer is the core component of AlphaFold2. It is a transformer-based architecture that takes as input the MSA representation and the pair representation and iteratively refines them. Its goal is to extract structural information from evolutionary data and encode it into \textit{a residue embedding} and  a \textit{residue-pair embeddings} that can later be converted into a three-dimensional structure by the Structure Module.
The Evoformer alternates between two types of operations:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Axial attention, which operates on the MSA representation.
    \item Triangular attention, which operates on the pair representation.
\end{enumerate}
\vspace{0.8em} \noindent
Axial attention is used to analyze the MSA. Since the MSA is a matrix whose rows are homologous sequences and whose columns are residue positions, attention can be applied along either dimension, asking for different questions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Row-wise attention: for a given, single sequence, attention asks: which residue positions are most relevant to each other? This is analogous to the attention mechanism in a language model, where attention identifies which words in a sentence are most relevant to each other. 
    \item Column-wise attention: for a given residue position, attention asks: which sequences in the MSA are most relevant to each other? Not all homologous 
    are equally informative. Some may be very similar to the target sequence, hence redundant, while others may be too divergent, hence noisy. Column-wise attention allows the network to weigh different sequences differently, effectively learning to focus on the most informative homologs.
\end{itemize}

\begin{wrapfigure}{r}{0.3\textwidth}
\includegraphics[width=0.28\textwidth]{figures/triangular_attention.png}
\end{wrapfigure}
\vspace{0.8em}\noindent
Triangular attention is instead applied on the \textit{pair} representation. Its purpose is to ensure that the predicted relationships between residues are compatible with a valid three-dimensional structure.
The key idea is that residue pairs cannot be considered independently. If residues $i$, $j$, and $k$ form a triangle, then the relationships between the pairs $(i,j)$, $(i,k)$, and $(j,k)$ must be mutually consistent. For example, if residues $i$ and $k$ are predicted to be close, and residues $k$ and $j$ are also predicted to be close, then residues $i$ and $j$ cannot be arbitrarily far apart. Otherwise, the predicted distances would violate the triangle inequality and could not correspond to any real 3D structure.
To address this problem, triangular attention treats the pair representation as a \textit{graph} in which residues are nodes and residue-pair relationships are edges. When updating a pair $(i,j)$, the model considers all triangles involving an intermediate residue $k$. In other words, the update of $(i,j)$ depends on related pairs such as $(i,k)$ and $(k,j)$.
This allows information to propagate through residue triplets and enables the network to reason about geometric constraints. Pairwise relationships that are inconsistent with the surrounding graph can therefore be corrected and refined.
In summary, triangular attention propagates geometric information between residue triplets, ensuring that the pair representation encodes a set of residue-residue relationships that can be embedded into a physically plausible three-dimensional structure. For this reason, it is often described as providing the network with a form of ``3D common sense''.




\paragraph{How does the Structure Module convert Evoformer representations into atomic coordinates?}

The Structure Module is the final component of AlphaFold2. Its task is to transform the abstract representations produced by the Evoformer into an explicit three-dimensional protein structure.
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/Af2_structuremodule.png}
\end{center}
The backbone is represented as a series of triangles: each residue is a triangle with the N, $C_\alpha$ and $C_\beta$ atoms as vertices. There is no enforcement that the residues
should be connected to each other, and as initialization they are all placed in the origin ($0,0,0$) of the $3$D space.
\smallskip \noindent
Then these triangles go through Invariant Point Attention (IPA) together with the output of the Evoformer. This is a specialized attention mechanism that
is able to process both pairwise relations and 3D spatial information. Each residue looks at other residues, taking into account both the learned embeddings and their current positions in 3D space. The result is a suggested movement.
This converts the abstract representations into concrete geometric transformations, allowing the network to iteratively refine the positions of the residues in three-dimensional space.

\smallskip \noindent
IPA is "Invariant"  in the sense that it is designed to be insensitive to global rotations and translations of the entire protein. This means that if the input structure is rotated or translated, the output will be transformed in the same way, preserving the relative geometry of the residues. This property is crucial for learning meaningful structural representations.

\smallskip \noindent
After several IPA iterations, each residue has: i) A position in space, ii) An orientation (a local coordinate system). Then backbone atoms $N,\quad C_\alpha,\quad C$
can be placed using standard protein geometry  (bond lengths and bond angles are essentially fixed).

From the resulting residue frames, AlphaFold predicts backbone coordinates and side-chain conformations. The final output is a complete set of atomic coordinates describing the three-dimensional structure of the protein.
Unlike AlphaFold1, which predicted geometric constraints and then relied on a separate optimization procedure, AlphaFold2 directly generates coordinates as part of the neural network itself. Thus, structure generation is fully integrated into the end-to-end learning process.

\smallskip \noindent
There is also a shallow ResNet which does the task of predicting the side-chain torsion angles ($\chi_1,\chi_2,\chi_3,\chi_4$, depending on the amino acid). These are the only degrees of freedom needed to place the side chain atoms, as the bond lengths and angles are fixed. 
Finally, a relaxation step can be applied to the predicted structure. This is a short \textit{molecular dynamics simulation} that removes any steric clashes and improves the stereochemistry of the model. 




\paragraph{What are the main strengths and limitations of AlphaFold2?}

Strengths:
\begin{itemize}
\item AlphaFold can predict protein structures based only on amino acid sequences:  it is a very good starting point for creating testable hypotheses
and therefore alphaFold models are routinely used as starting models for X-ray crystallography and cryo-EM
\item If available, AlphaFold can use templates from the PDB - this is a novelty with respect to AlphaFold1
\item AlphaFold produces confidence estimates for its predictions: confidence measure per amino acids (pLDDT) and predicted Aligned Error (PAE)
\end{itemize}
Limitations:
\begin{itemize}
\item AlphaFold does not model post-translational modifications, ligands, or cofactors. There is AlphaFill, a tool that can add ligands to AlphaFold models, but it is not part of the original AlphaFold pipeline.
\item AlphaFold is not reliable for predicting the effects of point mutations on protein structure or function.
\item AlphaFold (by default) predicts five models per run. However, these models are generally
very similar. In other words, AlphaFold usually cannot predict conformational variability in a
protein.
\item AlphaFold only accepts the $20$ standard amino acids as input.
\item  AlphaFold is not a molecular dynamics simulation and does not reproduce the physical folding pathway. It predicts the most likely final structure.
\end{itemize}



\paragraph{How does AlphaFold differ from Rosetta in its approach to protein structure prediction?}
Rosetta and AlphaFold represent two different paradigms.
\begin{description}
    \item[Rosetta:] Fragment assembly, Monte Carlo sampling, and statistical potentials derived from known structures.
    \item[AlphaFold:] End-to-end deep learning using sequence information, evolutionary covariation, attention mechanisms, and geometric reasoning.
\end{description}

The transition from Rosetta to AlphaFold represents the shift from manually designed scoring functions and sampling procedures to learned representations of protein sequence, evolution, and structure.
